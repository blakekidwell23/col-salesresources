We Don't Build Culture
With Posters on a Wall.
Culture is built by the decisions we make every day — the way we treat our people, serve our customers, and respond when things go wrong. These 12 principles are our decision-making framework.

A Message from Our CEO

When I became CEO, I spent a lot of time thinking about the kind of company we want to build. Not just the revenue we want to achieve or the profit we want to earn — but the reputation we want to have.

I believe great companies are built by people who share a common set of beliefs and standards. People who know what is expected of them, understand what the company stands for, and can make good decisions without waiting for permission.

These principles are not rules. They are not slogans. They are decision-making tools — the behaviors, standards, and beliefs that define who we are and who we aspire to become. They will evolve over time. But they represent the type of leadership, accountability, service, teamwork, and integrity that will make Collision Auto Parts successful for decades to come.

Because culture is not built by what leaders say. Culture is built by what leaders consistently do.

Blake Kidwell CEO, Collision Auto Parts
Why These Principles Exist
What they are not Rules to be enforced. Slogans to be posted. Policies to be filed away. These principles only matter if they change how we act.
What they are Decision-making tools. A common filter we all use when we face a choice about how to lead, serve, or respond. Every week, we discuss one together.
"When in doubt, ask yourself: Which decision best reflects our principles?"
The 12 Principles — Click any card to expand
1
Protect the Trust
Long-term trust beats short-term gain. Every time.
+
We do not sacrifice our reputation with employees, customers, vendors, or one another for temporary results. Trust is the most valuable asset we have — and it's the hardest to rebuild once spent.
Discussion Question
Where are we building trust? Where are we spending it?
2
Create Clarity
We do not assume understanding. We create it.
+
Great leaders explain the what, the why, and what success looks like. Confusion is not a team problem — it's a leadership problem. When people are unclear, they stall, guess, or disengage.
Discussion Question
What is one area where our team may be confused right now?
3
Own the Outcome
The scoreboard belongs to all of us.
+
We don't blame circumstances, departments, vendors, systems, or customers. We own results. Accountability is not about blame — it's about being the person who drives the outcome regardless of the obstacles.
Discussion Question
What result are we currently explaining instead of improving?
4
Earn the Next Order
Every customer decides every day whether we've earned their business.
+
Past success guarantees nothing. Every delivery, every quote, every phone call is an audition. The moment we assume loyalty, we start losing it. We earn the next order on every interaction.
Discussion Question
If our customers could grade us this week, what would they say?
5
Solve Problems, Don't Study Them
Speed matters. Action creates momentum.
+
We don't admire problems. We identify them, own them, and fix them. Analysis without action is just procrastination with a presentation attached. A good decision made quickly beats a perfect decision made too late.
Discussion Question
What problem have we been talking about too long?
6
Win as One Team
Departments don't win. Companies win.
+
Sales doesn't win. Operations doesn't win. Purchasing doesn't win. Collision Auto Parts wins — or we all lose together. When we optimize for our department at the expense of the company, we are choosing to lose slowly.
Discussion Question
Where are we optimizing for our department instead of the company?
7
Raise the Standard
What we tolerate becomes our standard.
+
Every exception eventually becomes a rule. When we look the other way on poor performance, bad behavior, or missed commitments, we are setting a new floor — not making an exception. We must have the courage to address problems quickly.
Discussion Question
What have we started accepting that we shouldn't?
8
Facts Over Feelings
Seek truth, even when it's uncomfortable.
+
Data informs decisions. Opinions matter. Facts matter more. We seek truth even when it challenges our assumptions, our instincts, or our current strategy. The most expensive decisions are the ones made on hope rather than reality.
Discussion Question
What belief are we holding that the data may not support?
9
Get Better Every Week
Small improvements create extraordinary results.
+
Nobody has to become 50% better overnight. Everyone should become 1% better every week. Compounded over a year, that's a different company — a different team. We are always in process, always improving, and we celebrate the progress as much as the result.
Discussion Question
What did we improve this week?
10
Leaders Go First
The standard starts with us.
+
Leaders model the behavior they expect. If we want accountability, transparency, communication, or urgency — it starts with leadership. We cannot ask our teams to rise to a standard we are not willing to live ourselves.
Discussion Question
What behavior am I asking others to demonstrate that I could model better?
11
Be the Partner
We succeed when our customers succeed.
+
We are not just a parts supplier. We are a partner in our customers' success. The more their shops thrive, the more our business grows. When we lead with their success in mind, we build relationships that outlast any competitor on price.
Discussion Question
How can we make our customers' lives easier this week?
12
Finish Strong
How we finish matters.
+
Anybody can start something. Great teams finish. The last impression is the lasting impression — with customers, with teammates, with results. We don't coast into the weekend, the quarter, or the year. We close hard.
Discussion Question
What commitment needs to be completed before we start something new?
"Build a company that employees are proud to work for, customers are proud to buy from, and partners are proud to support."
